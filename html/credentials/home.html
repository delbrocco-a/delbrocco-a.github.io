JSSF ~ Japan Super Science Fair 2019

During my time at Sixth Form, my school ran two trips, nick-named correspondingly "The Japan Trip", and "The CERN Trip", both of which were exclusive to students who took a science subject. There was also the rule that you could only really go on one of them, given the limited places availible on each. Besides the fact that the destination lay on the other hemishpere, and that the trip was partially subsidised by the Japanese State, I was also drawn to the application process. You couldn't just put your name in, and get chosen at random, you had to actually compete for your place, by presenting a project related to a current scientific inquiry that could be relevant to future developing industry. I liked this, not only because it treated students our age like adults, but also from that fact that a remarkable amount of the trip, gave control to it's students. You got to choose your project, time frame and to a great extent your itinery whilst you where there. At the time, and to this day, as much as I might have considered myself scientifically inclined, I was by far, never one of the top few of my year when it came down to it, but this was something new: it wasn't about how clever you were, it was about everything all at once. How you presented your work, what your ideas were, your leadership and application to your project. Despite getting a spot, and being one of the two projects to actually be persued, it project itself wasn't successful, but the idea behind it was. It taught me how to apply myself, the mistakes and logistics involved with material synthesis, as well as the personal nature of leading a group project, and how at the end of it all, the actual assets and success of anything involved with a community and a group, is all to do with the people that you're with, what they're inspired by, and the relevance of what your doing to them, and the people around you.
